An AI tutor that teaches from a textbook has a quiet failure mode: the book says one thing, the tutor says another. A student reads example 3 in section 5.2, asks the tutor about it, and gets a subtly different version: a sign flipped, a number that drifted when someone edited one copy and forgot the other. Most "AI plus content" products have two copies of the truth and hope they stay in sync. Algebra 1 Tutor has one, and it makes SymPy prove the math before anything ships.

That is the whole architecture in one sentence: the example you read in the book is byte-for-byte the example Claude pulls up when you ask about it, because neither one is the original. Both are generated from a single `curriculum.yaml`, and every computational answer in that file is re-derived by SymPy on every push.

**One source, four surfaces.** The course is a complete first algebra course: thirteen units, fifty-two lessons, from the language of algebra to a quadratics capstone plus a standalone statistics unit. None of it is the canonical artifact. The canonical artifact is the YAML. From that one source, the build fans out into four surfaces: the student-facing browser textbook, the tutor's own copy of the material, and matching study and tutor guides. Change the source, and all four regenerate together. There is no "update the textbook, then update the tutor" step where they can quietly drift apart, because there is nothing to update twice.

**The join key is a reference code.** Every example carries a stable id like `[5.2-EX3]`. That code is the load-bearing piece. It is printed in the browser textbook next to the worked example, and it is the handle the student hands to Claude: ask about `[5.2-EX3]` and the tutor pulls up the exact same example, not a paraphrase of it. The reference code turns "the tutor knows the textbook" from a vague claim into a guarantee. The page and the tutor agree because they are pointing at the same row in the same file.

**SymPy is the gate, not a spot check.** It would be easy to verify a handful of marquee problems and call it tested. The numbers here are stronger and more specific: 909 problems total, 614 of them auto-verified by SymPy, zero failures, enforced on every push through CircleCI. SymPy re-derives the answer to each computational problem from scratch and checks it against what the source claims. If the source says the slope through (1, 2) and (4, 11) is 3, SymPy recomputes (11 − 2) / (4 − 1) and refuses the build if it disagrees. The conceptual answers SymPy cannot check, the "explain why" problems, are reviewed by hand. The figures are computed too, not hand-drawn: every graph is generated from its equation, so the line labeled y = 2x − 1 passes through (0, −1) and (1, 1) by construction. CI also guards notation and byte-identical builds, so a rebuild from the same source can't silently produce different output.

**Why this is the interesting decision.** The instinct with an LLM tutor is to make the model smarter: better prompts, a bigger context window. This goes the other way. It makes the model's knowledge verifiable by pinning it to a source that a deterministic checker has already proven correct. The tutor is patient and pedagogically careful, but it is not the source of mathematical truth. SymPy is. That division of labor is the thing I would copy into the next project: let the language model do the teaching, and let a symbolic engine own the arithmetic, so a hallucinated answer can't reach a student because it never passed the gate.

**The tutor on top of the verified base.** Sitting above the source is the pedagogy, encoded as a Claude Agent Skill (a `SKILL.md` plus per-unit references) rather than improvised per session. The method is distilled from cited teaching research into one repeatable approach: hints arrive as questions on a graduated ladder so the student does the thinking; misconception diagnosis reads the specific wrong answer and repairs the cause rather than replying "try again"; concepts go concrete before symbolic, balance scales for equations and money for negatives before the notation; worked examples fade backward, showing the full solution and then handing over one more step each time. The tutor reads a photo of handwritten work and catches the slip behind a wrong answer, then verifies the fix. A short canonical session: a sign error on 8 − 2x = 14, caught and repaired, which is exactly where most people slip on a negative coefficient.

**Built to run on free Claude.** The constraint that shaped the engineering is that a good tutor should cost nothing. The whole experience targets Claude's free tier on Sonnet 4.6, and the rough edges of a stateless, no-code-execution session are engineered around rather than ignored. When code execution is off, it degrades gracefully instead of breaking mid-lesson. Progress Cards are a copy-paste resume token: paste one into a brand-new chat and pick up exactly where you left off. Because the tutor is prerequisite-aware, you can start at any unit and it patches the gaps behind you.

**Written by Claude, proven by SymPy.** None of the course is typed by hand. It began as deep research into how algebra is taught; Claude Opus turned that into the full textbook through custom content-generation skills; SymPy validated every example against the single source; then human testing and review; the rich figures built in HTML, with Nano Banana Pro (Google's Gemini 3 Pro Image) drawing the illustrative images. Fixes from Claude or an outside contributor run the same loop, research, generate, validate against SymPy, review, so the bar holds regardless of who made the change.

It is free and open source under permissive licenses (MIT for the skill and tooling, CC BY-NC 4.0 for content, CC0 for images). The reusable part is the machinery: a pedagogy encoded as a skill, examples locked to a SymPy-verified source, and a CI gate that catches drift between surfaces. Fork it, swap the curriculum, and point the same setup at a different subject.
